When interviewers ask you about items on your resume, your answers should: 

- tell an interesting story
- that hits multiple points
- concisely

In general, an interesting story grips the listener with conflict and its resolution.

A substantial problem arises, the protagonist grapples with some uncertainty, and ultimately solves the problem.

The conflict and resolution in an interesting story about software development will usually involve one or more of:

- an architectural choice
- a tool choice
- an algorithmic choice
- an especially-challenging requirement or constraint
- a difficult environment, often legacy
- a performance or reliability problem
- a difficult bug, perhaps non-deterministic

As the conflict and resolution of your development “war story” unfolds, you should naturally hit [multiple points that eng hiring managers are looking for](What%20are%20hiring%20managers%20looking%20for%200ef11ab064a1825e9e4f81e7c456bf0b.md).

Furthermore, you should do so *concisely*, showing that you are high-bandwidth in word and deed.

There’s a straightforward way to prep this: SAR outlines.

## SAR outlines

“SAR” stands for “Situation, Action, Result”.

These correspond to the beginning, middle, and end of your story.
