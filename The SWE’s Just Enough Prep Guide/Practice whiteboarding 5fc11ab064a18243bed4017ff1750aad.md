# Practice whiteboarding

<aside>
<img src="https://www.notion.so/icons/briefcase_blue.svg" alt="https://www.notion.so/icons/briefcase_blue.svg" width="40px" /> **This is Part 7 of the Just Enough Prep Guide.** This workbook will share some insights to assist in potential whiteboarding tests.

</aside>

“Whiteboarding” here refers strictly to using a physical or virtual whiteboard to hand-write / hand-draw as you solve a problem.

💡 *To emphasize the above: we are not talking about “whiteboarding problems” as the common negative metaphor for CS algorithm questions that many programmers associate with outdated or lazy interview practices. Here we literally mean using whiteboards as a medium.*

Writing out pseudocode and full implementations, organizing sample data into tables, drawing diagrams — all while fitting into the space constraints of a whiteboard — adds just one more thing to juggle and to distract you during the live interview.

Practicing on a whiteboard will substantially improve your performance when you are asked to interview in this medium.

This is true of live-coding problems and especially true for system design problems.

You may wonder why a team might ask you to hand-write solutions to a coding problem, when in real life we always use a code editor. Here’s [one representative explanation](https://qr.ae/pvOOdI):

> *As an interviewer, personally, I greatly prefer whiteboard interviews to laptop interviews. 

Coding on a whiteboard is much more interactive, the clumsiness prevents candidates from jumping right into the problem and instead forces them to think first and discuss the proposed solution. 

It also slows the candidate down enough to allow me to keep track of what they are doing. After all, I’m expected to know and understand what the candidate is doing at all times, catch and point out mistakes, and even take notes. It is really hard to keep track of what a good, fast candidate is doing on a laptop, and it’s much harder to steer the interview back onto a good path if the candidate ventures off into la-la-land.*
> 
> 
> *[Also] being able to explain an idea on a whiteboard is an important skill for a software developer. I regularly spend time in front of a whiteboard with my coworkers discussing ideas, drawing diagrams, code sketches etc. A candidate who can’t use a whiteboard is a clear no.*
>
