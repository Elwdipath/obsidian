In order to understand how to make a great engineering resume, recall [What are hiring managers looking for?](What%20are%20hiring%20managers%20looking%20for%200ef11ab064a1825e9e4f81e7c456bf0b.md) 

Now, your resume alone probably can’t deliver *all* of that information.  But it should directly demonstrate *some* of these points. And it should indirectly help with the other points by leaving tantalizing hooks for further discussion.

How to accomplish this? The “meat” of your resume is the items (often bullet-points) that describe a specific project or activity. Each item is a chance to directly demonstrate some of the above points.

Let’s look at some real examples.

## A first example

This example is from an engineer who works for a well-known company:

> Led design and build of Kafka consumers to process 2B+ leaderboard packets a day, with sub 40 millisecond latency.
> 

A convincing subset of vetting points are directly hit by this resume item:

Are all of your resume items hitting points like this?  

We want the hiring manager’s thought process to go something like: 

> The feature sounds business-important and challenging, with a need to scale to high usage (naive average from the given numbers == ~23,000 QPS) and thus a need for highly-optimized runtime performance. 

To “lead” the design, internal-evangelizing and introduction of a Kafka-based solution suggests initially proficiency in that specific technology. I.e., we assume this person didn’t just do a quick tutorial on Kafka, then build a large, important sub-system around it. That needs to be verified, but it’s a promising conversation starter…
>

## Your turn 👇

Revisit each item in your resume to decide if it tells a strong enough story. Usually you can tell because multiple points are hit at once.

Whereas if the story seems thin for a given item, consider simply removing it. Within reason, it’s better to have fewer, stronger items than to throw in filler/fluff.
